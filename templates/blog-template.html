The Art of Semantic HTML

Published on July 21, 2025 by Jane Doe

In the rush to build modern, interactive web applications, we've often forgotten the foundation upon which the web was built: clean, meaningful, semantic HTML.

Writing semantic HTML is not just an academic exercise; it's a practical approach that yields significant benefits in accessibility, SEO, and long-term maintainability. When you use the right tag for the right job, you're providing context that both browsers and assistive technologies can understand.

Why Semantics Matter

Consider the difference between <div class="button"> and <button>. While they can be styled to look identical, the latter comes with a wealth of built-in functionality. It's focusable, clickable with a keyboard, and announced as a "button" by screen readers. This is the power of semantics.

"The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect." — Tim Berners-Lee, Inventor of the World Wide Web

Practical Examples

Let's look at a few common patterns:

  • Navigation: Wrap your main site links in a <nav> element.
  • Main Content: The primary content of your page should be inside a <main> tag.
  • Self-Contained Content: A blog post, a product card, or a user comment should be an <article>.
